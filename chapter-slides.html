Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you should be able to:

  1. Describe the basic structure and mechanism of action of penicillins
  2. Explain the four mechanisms of bacterial resistance to β-lactams
  3. Classify β-lactamases by Ambler molecular class
  4. Differentiate the five classes of penicillins by spectrum
  5. Select appropriate penicillin therapy based on infection type and organism
  6. Recognize the role of β-lactamase inhibitors in clinical practice

This lecture covers approximately 1 hour of material. The learning objectives span from basic science (structure, mechanism) through clinical application (drug selection, dosing). Emphasize that penicillins remain cornerstone antibiotics despite resistance challenges.

Historical Context

  • 1928: Alexander Fleming discovers penicillin from Penicillium notatum
  • 1940s: Florey & Chain enable commercial production
  • Mid-1940s: Penicillin G available in UK and US
  • Initiated the modern antibiotic era

Fleming’s discovery was serendipitous—he noticed bacterial growth inhibition around a contaminating mold. The critical advance was Florey and Chain’s work to purify and mass-produce penicillin. This work earned them the 1945 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. Emphasize that despite being discovered nearly 100 years ago, penicillins remain essential antibiotics.

Penicillin Structure

Structure of penicillin showing the thiazolidine ring (1) and β-lactam ring (2). The site of β-lactamase attack opens the β-lactam ring.

The core structure consists of three parts: thiazolidine ring, β-lactam ring, and side chain (R group). The β-lactam ring is essential for activity—opening this ring by β-lactamases destroys antibacterial activity. The side chain determines spectrum and pharmacokinetics. Point out the carboxylic acid group—this makes penicillins acids and affects absorption.

Key Structural Elements

  • Thiazolidine ring — 5-membered ring containing sulfur
  • β-Lactam ring — 4-membered ring essential for activity
  • Side chain (R group) — Determines:
    • Antibacterial spectrum
    • Acid stability (oral absorption)
    • β-Lactamase resistance
    • Pharmacokinetic properties

The side chain modifications are what differentiate the various penicillin classes. For example, adding a bulky side chain near the β-lactam ring creates steric hindrance that protects against certain β-lactamases (antistaphylococcal penicillins). Adding an amino group (aminopenicillins) expands gram-negative coverage.

Mechanism of Action: Overview

Important

Key Concept: Penicillins inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs)

  • Target: Peptidoglycan synthesis — final cross-linking step
  • Effect: Weakened cell wall → osmotic lysis
  • Bactericidal (but not against non-growing cells)

The cell wall is essential for bacterial survival—it maintains shape and protects against osmotic pressure. Peptidoglycan is the main structural component. Penicillins are bactericidal because cell lysis is irreversible. However, cells that aren’t actively growing (stationary phase) are tolerant because they’re not synthesizing new cell wall.

Peptidoglycan Structure

PBP transpeptidation reaction cross-linking bacterial cell wall. NAG = N-acetylglucosamine; NAM = N-acetylmuramic acid.

Peptidoglycan is composed of long chains of alternating NAG-NAM disaccharides. Pentapeptide stems extend from NAM units. Cross-links between pentapeptides give the cell wall strength. The transpeptidation reaction that forms these cross-links is catalyzed by PBPs and is the target of penicillins. In gram-positive bacteria, the peptidoglycan layer is thick (50-100 molecules). In gram-negatives, it’s thin (1-2 molecules) but protected by the outer membrane.

Penicillin-Binding Proteins (PBPs)

PBP Function Effect of Inhibition
PBP1 Transpeptidase Cell lysis
PBP2 Cell elongation Round cells, lysis
PBP3 Cell division Filamentous cells
  • PBPs are serine proteases (related to β-lactamases!)
  • Bacteria have multiple PBP types
  • Different β-lactams have different PBP affinities

Understanding PBPs explains why different β-lactams have different effects on bacteria. The key insight is that β-lactamases evolved from PBPs—they share structural features. The difference is that PBPs bind penicillin tightly (slow deacylation = inhibition), while β-lactamases rapidly hydrolyze penicillin (fast deacylation = resistance). This evolutionary relationship is clinically important.

Four Mechanisms of Resistance

  1. β-Lactamase production — enzymatic destruction (most common)
  2. Decreased permeability — porin mutations in gram-negatives
  3. Efflux pumps — active drug removal
  4. Altered PBPs — low-affinity binding (e.g., MRSA’s PBP2a)

Warning

Multiple mechanisms often coexist, especially in Pseudomonas aeruginosa

β-Lactamase production is by far the most common mechanism. In gram-negatives, β-lactamases are located in the periplasmic space where they intercept β-lactams before they reach PBPs. Porin mutations reduce drug entry; efflux pumps expel drugs that do enter. Altered PBPs are key in gram-positives—MRSA produces PBP2a which has very low affinity for most β-lactams. These mechanisms are often additive.

β-Lactamases: The Primary Threat

  • Enzymes that hydrolyze the β-lactam ring
  • Located in periplasm (gram-negatives) or secreted (gram-positives)
  • Can be:
    • Chromosomal or plasmid-encoded
    • Constitutive or inducible
    • Narrow or broad spectrum

β-Lactamases are the most important resistance mechanism. Gram-negative bacteria concentrate their β-lactamases in the periplasmic space—this is strategically important because it puts the enzyme between the outer membrane and the PBPs. Gram-positives often secrete β-lactamases into the surrounding environment. The TEM-1 β-lactamase is the most common plasmid-encoded enzyme worldwide.

Ambler Classification of β-Lactamases

Class Active Site Representative Key Substrates
A Serine TEM, SHV, KPC, CTX-M Penicillins, cephalosporins
B Metallo (Zn) NDM, VIM, IMP All β-lactams except aztreonam
C Serine AmpC, CMY Cephalosporins
D Serine OXA Oxacillin, carbapenems

The Ambler classification is based on amino acid sequence similarity. Classes A, C, and D are serine β-lactamases that share evolutionary ancestry with PBPs. Class B metallo-β-lactamases use zinc ions and have a different mechanism. Clinically, this matters because β-lactamase inhibitors have different activity against different classes. For example, clavulanic acid works against class A but not class B or C enzymes.

Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamases (ESBLs)

  • Definition: Class A β-lactamases that hydrolyze extended-spectrum cephalosporins
  • Examples: CTX-M-15, TEM-24, SHV-12
  • Key features:
    • Plasmid-encoded (highly transmissible)
    • Inhibited by clavulanic acid
    • Often co-carry other resistance genes

Tip

Clinical Pearl: ESBLs are inhibited by clavulanic acid in vitro, but clinical outcomes with β-lactam/β-lactamase inhibitor combinations may be unpredictable

ESBLs are a major clinical problem worldwide. CTX-M-15 is now the dominant ESBL globally. These enzymes arose from point mutations in TEM and SHV that expanded their substrate spectrum. The clinical challenge is that while ESBLs are inhibited by clavulanic acid in the laboratory, treatment failures have been reported with β-lactam/β-lactamase inhibitor combinations for serious infections. Carbapenems have historically been preferred for serious ESBL infections.

Carbapenemases: The Greatest Threat

Class A (KPC)

  • Serine enzyme
  • Hydrolyzes all β-lactams
  • Inhibited by avibactam
  • Endemic in US

Class B (NDM, VIM)

  • Metallo-enzyme (requires Zn)
  • Hydrolyzes all β-lactams except aztreonam
  • NOT inhibited by current inhibitors
  • Global spread

Carbapenemases represent the most concerning resistance mechanism because they compromise our “last-resort” antibiotics. KPC (Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase) is endemic in the US and can be combated with newer inhibitors like avibactam. NDM (New Delhi metallo-β-lactamase) and other class B enzymes are more challenging because no approved inhibitor works against them. The “aztreonam loophole”—that metallo-β-lactamases cannot hydrolyze aztreonam—is being exploited clinically.

Classification of Penicillins

  1. Natural penicillins: Penicillin G, Penicillin V
  2. Penicillinase-resistant: Nafcillin, Oxacillin, Dicloxacillin
  3. Aminopenicillins: Ampicillin, Amoxicillin
  4. Carboxypenicillins: Ticarcillin (largely obsolete)
  5. Ureidopenicillins: Piperacillin (used with tazobactam)

This classification is based on antibacterial spectrum. Each class represents modifications to the side chain that alter activity. Natural penicillins have the narrowest spectrum but highest activity against susceptible organisms. Penicillinase-resistant penicillins have bulky side chains that protect against staphylococcal β-lactamases. Aminopenicillins add gram-negative coverage. Antipseudomonal penicillins (carboxypenicillins and ureidopenicillins) have the broadest spectrum.

Natural Penicillins: Spectrum

Most Active Against:

  • Streptococcus spp. (including S. pneumoniae†, S. pyogenes)
  • Neisseria meningitidis
  • Treponema pallidum (syphilis)
  • Anaerobes (except Bacteroides fragilis)
  • Listeria monocytogenes

† Penicillin-susceptible strains only

Natural penicillins remain the drugs of choice for several important infections. Penicillin G remains first-line for syphilis. For streptococcal infections, resistance is rare (though pneumococcal resistance is a concern). The narrow spectrum can be advantageous—less collateral damage to normal flora. Penicillin G is given IV; penicillin V can be given orally but is less bioavailable.

Penicillinase-Resistant Penicillins

Spectrum: Methicillin-susceptible Staphylococcus spp. (MSSA)

Drug Route Key Features
Nafcillin IV Hepatic metabolism; causes hypokalemia
Oxacillin IV Hepatotoxicity risk
Dicloxacillin PO Highest protein binding (96%)

Warning

NOT active against MRSA — MRSA has altered PBP2a with low affinity

These agents are specifically designed to resist staphylococcal β-lactamases. The bulky side chains create steric hindrance that prevents β-lactamase access to the β-lactam ring. However, they are completely ineffective against MRSA because MRSA resistance is not due to β-lactamases but to an altered PBP. For serious MSSA infections (endocarditis, osteomyelitis), nafcillin or oxacillin are preferred over vancomycin because they’re more effective.

Aminopenicillins

Aminopenicillins: Ampicillin and Amoxicillin structures. Note the amino group (NH₂) on the side chain.

The key structural feature is the amino group on the α-carbon of the side chain. This increases activity against gram-negatives by improving outer membrane penetration. Amoxicillin has an additional hydroxyl group that improves oral absorption. Both are susceptible to β-lactamases, so they’re usually combined with inhibitors for broader coverage.

Aminopenicillins: Clinical Use

Ampicillin (IV preferred):

  • Meningitis: Listeria, Group B strep
  • Enterococcal infections (E. faecalis)
  • Combined with sulbactam for broader coverage

Amoxicillin (oral):

  • Otitis media (high-dose for resistant pneumococci)
  • Community-acquired pneumonia
  • H. pylori eradication
  • Combined with clavulanate for broader coverage

Oral amoxicillin is preferred over ampicillin because of better absorption (74-80% vs 33-54%). High-dose amoxicillin (80-90 mg/kg/day in children) achieves concentrations that overcome penicillin-resistant pneumococci, making it first-line for otitis media. For adult pneumonia, standard-dose amoxicillin is recommended for outpatients without comorbidities. The combination products expand coverage to include β-lactamase-producing organisms.

Piperacillin-Tazobactam

  • Piperacillin: Broadest spectrum ureidopenicillin
    • Pseudomonas aeruginosa coverage
    • Excellent anaerobic coverage
    • Good activity against Enterobacterales
  • Tazobactam: β-lactamase inhibitor
    • Restores activity against class A enzymes
    • Does NOT inhibit AmpC or metallo-enzymes

Tip

Clinical Pearl: Standard dose is 3.375g or 4.5g q6-8h; extended infusions (over 4 hours) maximize time above MIC

Piperacillin-tazobactam is one of the most commonly used broad-spectrum antibiotics. The combination provides coverage against most common hospital pathogens except MRSA and organisms with AmpC or carbapenemases. Extended infusion dosing (infusing over 4 hours rather than 30 minutes) has become standard practice because β-lactam efficacy depends on time above MIC. This allows the same total daily dose to achieve better pharmacodynamic targets.

Sulbactam Ampicillin ✓ ✗ ✗ ✗

This table summarizes the key differences between traditional and newer β-lactamase inhibitors. The traditional inhibitors (clavulanate, sulbactam, tazobactam) are β-lactams themselves and work as “suicide inhibitors.” The newer agents (avibactam, vaborbactam) have different mechanisms and broader activity. Note that NO approved inhibitor works against class B metallo-β-lactamases—this is a major unmet need.

Pharmacokinetics: Key Points

  • Oral absorption varies dramatically:
    • Penicillin V: 60% (unaffected by food)
    • Ampicillin: 30-55% (decreased by food)
    • Amoxicillin: 74-92% (unaffected by food)
  • Protein binding: 17% (aminopenicillins) to 97% (dicloxacillin)
  • Elimination: Primarily renal (except nafcillin → hepatic)
  • Half-life: Short (0.5-1.5 hours) → requires frequent dosing

The short half-lives of penicillins necessitate frequent dosing (every 4-6 hours) for serious infections. This can be addressed with extended infusions or continuous infusions for critically ill patients. Renal dosing adjustments are needed for most penicillins except nafcillin (hepatic elimination) and the antistaphylococcal penicillins. Probenecid can prolong half-life by blocking tubular secretion but is rarely used clinically.

The antistaphylococcal penicillins (nafcillin, oxacillin, dicloxacillin) do not require renal adjustment because they’re primarily eliminated by the liver. All other penicillins accumulate in renal failure and require dose reduction. For hemodialysis patients, dosing should occur after dialysis to avoid rapid drug removal. Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) dosing is complex and depends on the specific modality and settings used.

Adverse Effects: Hypersensitivity

  • Most important adverse effect
  • Range: Rash → Anaphylaxis
  • Penicilloyl derivatives = major determinants
  • True anaphylaxis is rare (<0.01%)
  • History of “penicillin allergy” often inaccurate
    • ~2% will react if challenged

Tip

Clinical Pearl: Skin testing and oral challenges can safely de-label most patients with reported penicillin allergy

Penicillin allergy is one of the most over-reported drug allergies. Many patients labeled as “penicillin allergic” either never had a true allergy or have lost their sensitivity over time (IgE-mediated allergies often wane). This matters because patients labeled as penicillin allergic receive broader-spectrum, more toxic, and more expensive alternatives. Penicillin allergy de-labeling programs using skin testing and graded oral challenges are safe and effective.

Other Adverse Effects

  • Neurologic: Seizures (high doses, renal failure)
  • Hematologic: Neutropenia, platelet dysfunction
  • GI: Nausea, diarrhea, C. difficile infection
  • Renal: Interstitial nephritis (methicillin, nafcillin)
  • Hepatic: Elevated transaminases, cholestatic jaundice
  • Electrolyte: Hypokalemia (nafcillin), hyperkalemia (K⁺ penicillin G)

These adverse effects are generally dose-dependent or idiosyncratic. Neurotoxicity occurs with very high doses (>20 million units/day of penicillin G) and renal impairment. Nafcillin can cause significant hypokalemia, especially with prolonged therapy. The penicillinase-resistant penicillins are associated with interstitial nephritis and hepatotoxicity. GI effects are common with oral penicillins, particularly ampicillin.

Clinical Application: Case 1

55-year-old man with cellulitis

  • No systemic symptoms
  • No risk factors for MRSA
  • No drug allergies

Question: What is the optimal oral therapy?

Answer: Dicloxacillin or cephalexin (targeting MSSA and streptococci)

This case illustrates selection of a narrow-spectrum agent for uncomplicated skin infection. Without risk factors for MRSA (prior MRSA, recent hospitalization, injection drug use), empiric coverage should target MSSA and streptococci. Dicloxacillin is an excellent choice—oral, narrow-spectrum, and highly effective. Alternative would be a first-generation cephalosporin. Avoid broader agents like amoxicillin-clavulanate unless there’s reason to suspect polymicrobial infection.

Clinical Application: Case 2

68-year-old woman with pneumonia

  • Community-acquired, outpatient treatment
  • COPD, no recent antibiotics
  • No drug allergies

Question: What is appropriate empiric therapy?

Answer: Amoxicillin 1g TID (or amoxicillin-clavulanate if concern for H. influenzae)

For community-acquired pneumonia in an outpatient without comorbidities, high-dose amoxicillin is guideline-recommended first-line therapy. It covers the most common pathogen (S. pneumoniae) including strains with reduced penicillin susceptibility. If the patient has COPD or recent antibiotic exposure (risk factors for H. influenzae), amoxicillin-clavulanate would be preferred to cover potential β-lactamase-producing strains. This case emphasizes appropriate antibiotic stewardship—using narrow-spectrum agents when possible.

Clinical Application: Case 3

45-year-old man with complicated intra-abdominal infection

  • Post-operative abscess
  • Recent hospitalization
  • Question: What empiric therapy covers gram-negatives, anaerobes, and potential resistance?

Answer: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g q6h (extended infusion) (or carbapenem if ESBL suspected)

This case illustrates the use of broad-spectrum β-lactam/β-lactamase inhibitor therapy for serious polymicrobial infections. Piperacillin-tazobactam covers Enterobacterales, Pseudomonas, and anaerobes including B. fragilis. Extended infusion optimizes pharmacodynamics. If the patient has risk factors for ESBL-producing organisms (prior ESBL colonization, recent travel to endemic areas), a carbapenem might be preferred. This highlights the importance of knowing local resistance patterns and patient risk factors.

Summary: Key Takeaways

  1. β-Lactam ring is essential for activity; its hydrolysis = resistance
  2. β-Lactamases are the most common resistance mechanism
  3. Classification: Natural → Antistaphylococcal → Amino → Antipseudomonal
  4. Match the spectrum to the pathogen and site of infection
  5. β-Lactamase inhibitors extend coverage but have limitations
  6. Most “penicillin allergies” can be safely de-labeled

These are the core concepts students should retain. Emphasize the relationship between structure and function—understanding why the β-lactam ring is essential helps students understand resistance mechanisms and drug selection. The principle of matching spectrum to pathogen is fundamental to antibiotic stewardship. Finally, penicillin allergy de-labeling is an important clinical opportunity to improve patient care.

Questions for Discussion

  1. Why might a patient with an ESBL-producing E. coli bacteremia fail treatment with piperacillin-tazobactam despite in vitro susceptibility?

  2. A patient needs treatment for MSSA endocarditis but has documented IgE-mediated penicillin allergy. What are your options?

  3. Why do metallo-β-lactamases spare aztreonam but hydrolyze carbapenems?

These discussion questions reinforce key concepts. Question 1 addresses the unpredictable clinical efficacy of β-lactam/BLI combinations against ESBLs (inoculum effect, enzyme hyperproduction). Question 2 prompts discussion of desensitization protocols or alternative agents (cefazolin with skin testing, vancomycin, daptomycin). Question 3 explores the structural basis of β-lactamase specificity—the monobactam structure of aztreonam doesn’t fit the metallo-enzyme active site.
